What is a ledger stone?

In the porches, chapels, aisles and crossing you will see these stones, carved with names and dates. Every ledger stone (from legen “to place, lay”) recalls one of the dead remembered at St. Nicholas’. You had to be important to have a ledger stone. The more money you paid, the closer to the altar you lay. If really rich, you might have had a related monument on the wall, or your own chapel. In contrast, poor people were buried outside, sometimes in unmarked graves.













Ledger stones are often decorated with family coats of arms and inscriptions. Sometimes, at a later date, people paid a letter cutter to have their name added to someone else’s stone.

The 2020 renovations revealed many additional stones and names, previously covered in limewash by the Victorians. These memorials of great beauty are a significant historical record.

George Ord

Born
1699

Died
1769

Family
Father: Thomas Ord
Mother: Ann
Siblings: Unknown
Spouse: Mary Mitchell (possibly a second marriage)
Children: Unknown

Guild Links
Wax Chandlers
Barber Surgeons

Occupation
Wax Chandler

At 15 years’ old, George was apprenticed to Robert Heslop, a Barber Surgeon and Wax Chandler. By 1721, he had his own business. As wax chandler George Ord made candles, torches and seals from bees wax. Bees wax candles were more expensive than tallow and smelt better, so they were popular in churches and grand homes. George ran his own business and employed an apprentice, William Hewson. Perhaps George and William made church candles for St Nicholas?

Oil and steel manufacturer

Businessman Richard Chambers traded seeds, oils and saddlery. Educated at Newcastle’s Royal Grammar School, Chambers went on to become Sheriff of Newcastle in 1784 and an alderman in 1795. Chambers invested early in the Pilgrim Street bank, founded with close friend and business partner David Landell. Nicknamed ‘Nabob’s Bank’, it had strong family links with the colonialist East India Company. Chambers’ investments went awry and in 1798 he was declared bankrupt.

1801

Father: Ralph Heron
Mother: Unknown

Guild Links

Occupation
Attorney

Lawyer Ralph Heron was no stranger to drama. Ralph eloped with Ann Cuthbertson, the Town Clerk’s daughter, to be married at Gretna Green in 1763. Three of their eight children suffered fatal accidents. Mariners George and Charles died at sea off India. 18-year-old Ralph junior died in a Lunardi hot air balloon accident on the Town Moor in 1786.

Ralph was clearly accepted back into society after eloping. He was Bailiff of Hexham for over 30 years and three times Under Sheriff of Northumberland. The Herons were a wealthy family with links back to the Border Reivers. Ralph’s parents Frances and Ralph are also interred at St Nicholas’. Frances’s ledger stone came to light in the 2020 renovations.

Nathaniel Ellison overcame a scurrilous family challenge and died a well-respected man. Nathaniel attended Newcastle’s Royal Grammar School and Merton College Oxford, before becoming a vicar. His church roles included curate at St Andrew’s, Newcastle, vicar of Bolam, perpetual curate of Doddington, vicar of Bedlington and private chaplain to the Earl of Tankerville. All those posts gave him a comfortable living. And the scandal? Nathaniel’s father John was mainly remembered for his portrait appearance in a satirical pamphlet, priced 1/6, that lampooned John Ellison and his many influential friends.

Isaac Cookson

Born
1679

Died
1743 (Ledger stone says 1744)

Family
Father: unknown
Mother: unknown
Siblings: unknown
Spouse: Hannah Buston
Children: John, Joseph, Isaac, Sarah and Hannah

Guild Links
None known

Occupation
Industrialist – Glass, coal, alum, silver, lead and iron

Isaac Cookson founded one of Newcastle’s most successful industrialist families. Born in Cumbria, he moved to Newcastle and took over the Dagnias’ Flint Glasshouse at Close Gate, then the Crown Glassworks. Isaac invested in businesses that supported glassmaking – salt and alum refineries, coal and lead mines and iron-making. The Cooksons owned works at Newcastle, South Shields, Chester-le-Street, Hexham, Cumberland and North Yorkshire.

The Cooksons were chapel-going Unitarians, but Isaac is buried in St Nicholas’ as a prestigious man of Newcastle. Some Cooksons even held public office, officially forbidden to Nonconformists, unless they married a member of the Church of England. Isaac’s ledger stone gives the wrong year of death, perhaps due to bygone Church calendar customs.

Henry lived a quiet life, compared to his notorious father, John. Henry’s wife Barbara came from a wealthy merchant family. The couple had a good house with four chimneys in the Close Gate area and traded in coals. Henry was thrown out of the Town Council during Oliver Cromwell’s Commonwealth, due to his father’s ‘zealous persecution of Puritans’. He was later reinstated, but the humiliation would have rankled. Famously, Henry’s father John Marlay saved the Lantern Tower during the Siege of Newcastle. He shut Scots prisoners into the tower to deter the Scots from exploding it.

Occupation
Chemist and druggist

James Walker lived and worked on the Tyne Bridge, next door to the Hancocks, whose names appear on the same stone. Druggist James prepared remedies himself and may have been apprenticed as an apothecary. The Great Flood of 1771 probably disrupted his shop, but the north side of the bridge suffered less than the south, and James claimed no compensation for damage. Why James is commemorated here is a mystery. He did not attend St Nicholas’, held no public office and was not rich. Perhaps the Hancocks paid for his name as a mark of close friendship, for they named one of their sons Henry Walker after their friend.

Reluctant businessman John Hancock was a keen naturalist and collector. He counted naturalist and engraver Thomas Bewick of Amen Corner among his good friends. John died young, leaving wife Jane with six children aged under 8 and a business to run. Jane guarded his work until the children were old enough to follow their parents’ passion for studying nature. Son John founded the Hancock Museum (now the Great North Museum) to house his brother Albany’s collections. While John senior and Jane Hancock rest at St Nicholas’, Albany Hancock and siblings John, Eleanor and Mary Jane are in Old Jesmond Cemetery.

Widowed at 34 with six children under 8, Jane Hancock took over the family business and made it flourish. Jane championed her children’s education and encouraged them to pursue their passions for art and nature, with outings to collect fossils and other specimens. She owned houses at Windmill Hill, Gateshead and St Mary’s Terrace in town, where you can now see a plaque commemorating her sons, John and Albany. While son Thomas worked in the family business, John founded the Hancock Museum (Great North Museum) to house Albany’s collections. Sisters Eleanor and Mary Jane collaborated with and supported their brothers. Mary Jane was a respected artist in her own right.

George Davidson

Born
1714

Died
1776

Family
Father: unknown
Mother: unknown
Siblings: unknown
Spouse: Alice Cunningham
Children: John

Guild Links
None known

Occupation
Tobacconist

George and Alice Davidson ran a tobacconist on The Side. When George was just 10, the Partis tobacconist’s shop on The Side exploded when gunpowder, stored in the cellar, blew up. Twelve people died in the explosion, over a hundred were injured and one man was blown onto St Nicholas’ church roof.

The Davidsons had a warehouse as well as a shop, so perhaps imported tobacco at scale. After George died, widow Alice ran the business until their son turned 21. Alice left money to family members, and a weekly income and her feather beds to her favourite servants. The family may have been chapel-goers, for her executors were Unitarians.

Saddler David Landell is one of numerous chapel-going Nonconformists remembered in the ledger stones of St Nicholas’. Landell attended the Hanover Street Unitarian Chapel and even painted the meeting house. Guild records show that Landell did not attend meetings throughout the 1750s and 60s. Normally this would attract fines – which often paid for the upkeep of the church – but David was not fined or sanctioned. Perhaps he was unwell, for he made a will in 1748 and took on two nephews as apprentices.

Upholder (upholsterer)

At 12, Bartholomew Kent began his apprenticeship with master upholsterer William Smith on The Side, Newcastle. At 19 he moved to London. He returned with a flourish as heir to William Smith’s business, advertising all the latest fabrics and styles from London. In his 30s, Bartholomew married Smith’s widowed daughter, Dorothy. She died during childbirth in 1764.

In the 1770s, The Side was lined with upholsterers, milliners, goldsmiths and merchants. Bartholomew supplied Trinity House and Low Lights at North Shields, but never sought public office himself. His nephew Walton is memorialised on the north wall of the Cathedral.

James Clavering lived at 28-30 The Close, now the House of Tides. Born into an influential family, he apprenticed to merchant Roger Nicholson and married the boss’s daughter, Grace. Born
1669

Died
1735

Family
Father: Robert Mallabar
Mother: Alice Mallabar
Siblings: unknown
Spouse: John Clavering
Children Alice and Elizabeth. John and James died in childhood

Guild Links
None

Occupation
Coal mining business
Trader

Born into the wealthy merchant Mallabar family, Jane inherited £400 from her father and invested cannily in property and co-ownership of a coal business with her brothers and step-brothers. Both her mother Alice and grandmother were successful businesswomen. Jane had an excellent education and clear examples of how to succeed in a male-dominated world. She married her colleague John Clavering and became Dame Lady Jane.

Jane took a strong lead in the business and at times faced controversy. She nearly ended up in prison, after the company tried to gain a trade advantage by controlling the local coal supply wagonways, but was sentenced to seven days’ house arrest in her palatial London home.

Dame Lady Jane gave generously to St Nicholas’ and bought 3 burial plots here. Her daughters inherited the family wealth and married into the aristocracy.

Nicholas Ridley moved to Newcastle at 14 to serve a 9-year apprenticeship as a Merchant Adventurer, the wealthier class of merchant. Successful textiles man James Bell was churchwarden at St Nicholas’ for several years but kept out of town politics. Bell began as a leather tanner and dealer on The Side but branched out into felt making. Felting and tanning both need lots of water and Bell took on a works in County Durham, which also produced paper. He later took properties at Sandgate and High Bridge, probably for the rent income. Occupation

This beautiful stone was discovered under a carpet in 2020 and is the Cathedral’s oldest known ledger stone. The Latin inscription names the Stote family of Stote’s Hall in Jesmond and their heraldic crest appears. Edward Stote paid for the stone to honour his parents, Eleanor and the elder Richard, a successful merchant.

The younger Richard was Edward’s son. He was a successful lawyer, Sergeant-at-Law and a Commissioner for King Charles II, meaning he raised funds locally for the king – no mean task given Charles II’s extravagant lifestyle.

Edward Stote does not appear on the stone. Edward served King Charles I. As a member of Newcastle Common Council, he would have co-signed the surrender to General Leslie during the Siege of Newcastle in 1644.

Occupation

The first Dagnias arrived in Newcastle as Italian glassmakers in the early 1600s. They set up a successful glassworks at Close Gate, making four kinds of glass. Their flint glass included their famous Newcastle Baluster and much of the tableware was engraved at Beilby’s workshop at Amen Corner in St Nicholas’ precinct. Generations later, John Dagnia was a cashier at Newcastle’s Old Bank. ‘Gentleman’ John left £600 in his will. His wife Elizabeth left some £2,500, a large property and a share in a ship. Was she independently wealthy or was John a ‘creative accountant’?

Visit the Laing Art Gallery to see Dagnia glass.

Wealthy William Carr was probably a covert Roman Catholic. His social standing saw him buried at St Nicholas’. William was a Merchant Adventurer. Membership of that guild gave him automatic rights to join the Russia Company and Eastland Company, opening doors to trade freely all over Europe and the Baltic. His wife Jane Cocke came from a prominent local family, all active in local politics. The couple were well-connected. Carr had shares in coal and lead mines, salt pans and ships. He owned property and imported luxury goods from London. The clue to his Catholic sympathies lies in the way his will is written with elaborate religious language.

William and his wife Jane are commemorated in the border of this ledger stone. 138 years after William’s death, the centre of the stone was reused, this time to remember John Dagnia.

Occupation
Master Mariner

Thomas Loggan married Margaret Bayles at St Nicholas’ in 1780 and shares this stone with his wife and eminent father-in-law. A Master Mariner, Thomas endured all the hardships and dangers of life at sea. He sailed as a whaler and privateer (so not much better than a pirate), and part-owned ships including the Heart of Oak, the Kingston and several whaling ships named Peggy or Polly. Loggan navigated the icy waters of the Arctic Circle to hunt right whales, fin whales and dolphins. Despite Thomas’s long absences, Margaret had one son, who became quay master and Sheriff of Newcastle.

Nathaniel Bayles

Born
1717

Died
1791

Family
Father: unknown
Mother: unknown
Siblings: unknown
Spouse: Ann Hanby
Children: five children, four died in childhood

Guild Links
Barber Surgeons
Wax and Tallow Chandlers
Peruke Makers (wig makers)

Occupation
Master Mariner

Orphaned at 11, Nathaniel Bayles inherited money and became a surgeon, who treated the poor without charge. He was a key player in securing Newcastle Town Moor as common land for the townsfolk and took vicar James Lushington to court for ‘misappropriation of funds’ or fiddling the books. Bayles was Swordbearer to the Newcastle Corporation for 45 years, yet his business interests took in slavery, whaling and warfare. He invented the first 3-pronged whaling harpoon, granted patent by King George III. He part-owned the Providence, which sailed as a scientific research ship and whaler, then a troop ship in the American War of Independence and later transported enslaved people.

Nathaniel and Ann lived in St Nicholas’ churchyard near their friend, the engraver Thomas Bewick. Nathaniel’s will requested a ‘decent secure burial’. As a surgeon, he would have seen the bodies that were dug up and sold on to trainee doctors and scientists.

Rev James Stephen Lushington

Born
1734

Died
1801

Family
Father: unknown
Mother: unknown
Siblings: unknown
Spouse: Mary Law (1st),
Mary Christian (2nd)
Children: nine children, three died in childhood

Guild Links
None

Occupation
Vicar of Newcastle for 19 years

There’s a whiff of scandal around Revd James Lushington. Look for his name squeezed onto someone else’s ornate memorial stone. During the 1780s renovations of St Nicholas’, he would have known of ledger stones being auctioned off. Seven members of the congregation later took Lushington to court for ‘misappropriation of funds’. The case took three years, but the verdict is unknown.

Lushington’s wealthy background took him to Charterhouse School and Peterhouse College, Cambridge. He married a bishop’s daughter and secured numerous church roles, which often took him away from Newcastle. Curate John Ellison managed St Nicholas’ during Lushington’s frequent absences and Ellison lived in the vicarage. Lushington served as Justice of the Peace for Northumberland and Chaplain to General Yorke’s Dragoons, as well as Vicar of Newcastle. He laid the foundation stone of All Saints, Newcastle in 1786.

Robert Mallabar

Born
1634

Died
1676

Family
Father: unknown
Mother: unknown
Siblings: unknown
Spouse: Alice Proctor
Children: George, Robert and little Jane

Guild Links
Merchant Adventurers

Occupation
Merchant

Merchant Adventurer Robert Mallabar was Alice Mallabar’s second husband after Roger Proctor. Robert was a regular at St Nicholas’ and the couple married here. In 1679 Robert was churchwarden to curate Ralph Astell. Perhaps the family friendship and seeing Alice’s involvement in business encouraged the thinker and writer Mary Astell, an early advocate of women’s rights. Robert Mallabar was well-respected and served as alderman, assistant governor of the Guild of Merchant Adventurers and Sheriff of Newcastle. He died young, perhaps of the Jolly Rant, a flu that killed over 900 people in Newcastle in 1675-6. For a second time, widow Alice took on the business and made it flourish. Robert and Alice’s daughter, Jane Mallabar, became coal magnate, Dame Lady Jane Clavering.

Occupation
Merchant

Alice Swinburne learned business from her widowed coal merchant mother. After marriage, Alice was widowed twice and each time took on the family business and made it work. Alice made the deals, balanced the books, trained and managed her apprentices, secured advantageous apprenticeships for her sons and ensured all her children were well-educated, including the girls. She raised daughter Jane to become the fiercely intelligent and strong-willed businesswoman, Dame Lady Jane Clavering. Alice and Robert had a large house with garden and summer house, and owned properties including warehouses for whalebone and flax. When Robert died without making a will, Alice did the legal work to secure the properties, as well as running the family businesses and raising her children.

Occupation
Merchant Adventurer

Roger grew up in a brewing and baking family. He was an unusually mature 16 when he began his apprenticeship. On completion, he became a Freeman of the Guild of Merchant Adventurers, and therefore the Eastland Company too. He would have done business with clients and traders all over northern Europe and the Baltic. Roger gets two mentions in the Merchant Adventurers Court record – first for seizing a dodgy consignment of flax brought in by a would-be tax-evader, and second for cursing fellow merchant, Gilbert Dobson, though Dobson was known as a nasty piece of work. He died at 36 and widow Alice took on running the family warehouses, cellars and shop, managing the apprentices and further building the business.
